A beginner's guide to Formula 1

What is Formula 1?

The Formula One series originated with the European Championship of Grand Prix motor racing of the 1920s and 1930s. The formula consists of a set of rules that all participants' cars must meet. Formula One was a new formula agreed upon during 1946 with the first non-championship races taking place that year. The first Formula 1 race was the 1946 Turin Grand Prix. A number of Grand Prix racing organisations had laid out rules for a world championship before World War II, but due to the suspension of racing during the conflict, the World Drivers' Championship did not become formalised until 1947. The first world championship race took place at Silverstone in the United Kingdom in 1950.

During the 2000s, Formula One cars had become complex, efficient, and reliable, so much that actual racing between drivers began to become less common. 2009 saw the introduction of new rules and regulations (including engine RPM limits, an adjustable front wing, and disproportionate wing sizes) encouraging overtaking. In 2011, other changes included a re-introduction KERS, the introduction of the Drag reduction system (DRS) (a moveable flap on the cars rear wing), a change in tyre supplier to Pirelli, and a ban on team orders. Red Bull dominated from 2010 to 2013, with Sebastian Vettel winning four Driver's Championships.

Formula One entered its second turbocharged era in 2014. The series is being run under the most radical engine regulation changes since 1995. All cars entering a Formula One championship race must run with 1.6-litre single turbocharged 6-cylinder engines. Since 2017, new rules about aerodynamics and the improvement of the engines, reaching around 1000 hp, have made the cars faster than the 2004 ones, breaking numerous track records. Mercedes and their drivers, Lewis Hamilton and Nico Rosberg dominated the start of the hybrid era, the success being Mercedes' development of the split turbocharger, giving Mercedes advantage due to the need for less cooling and a more compact power unit. This domanance came to an end in 2021 when Red Bull's Max Verstappen won the Driver's Championship.
